# USB Microphone Selection Guide for Xiangqi Commentary

A comprehensive guide to choosing recording equipment for xiangqi (Chinese chess) content creation. Covers USB microphones across every budget tier, webcam options, and a complete starter kit recommendation with setup tips tailored to chess commentary.

---

## 1. Budget Tier ($20–50)

If you are just starting out and want to test the waters before committing serious money, these microphones deliver surprisingly capable audio for the price.

### Fifine K669B (~$25) — Best Budget Pick

- **Polar pattern:** Cardioid (front-facing, rejects rear noise).
- **Connection:** USB-A, true plug-and-play — no drivers required on Windows, Mac, or Linux.
- **Sample rate:** 16-bit / 48 kHz.
- **Build:** All-metal body, integrated desk stand with adjustable angle.
- **Volume control:** Physical gain knob on the mic body.
- **Why it works for xiangqi:** Solo commentary is the most common format, and cardioid is ideal for a single speaker in a quiet room. The K669B captures clear, intelligible voice without picking up keyboard clicks or fan noise from behind. At $25, you can start recording today and upgrade later if the channel grows.
- **Downsides:** The included desk stand transmits vibrations (typing, bumping the desk). No headphone monitoring jack. Sounds noticeably "thinner" than the mid-range options.

### TONOR TC-777 (~$30) — Good for Quiet Rooms

- **Polar pattern:** Cardioid.
- **Connection:** USB-A, plug-and-play.
- **Sample rate:** 16-bit / 48 kHz.
- **Extras:** Ships with a small tripod stand, pop filter, and shock mount adapter.
- **Why it works for xiangqi:** The included accessories mean you spend nothing extra to get started. In a quiet home office, the TC-777 sounds clean and warm enough for commentary.
- **Downsides:** More sensitive to ambient noise than the K669B. The tripod is flimsy. Not well-suited to noisy environments.

### Pros and Cons for Xiangqi Use (Budget Tier)

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Extremely low cost of entry | Noticeable quality gap vs. mid-range |
| No drivers, no setup complexity | Desk stands pick up vibrations |
| Good enough for solo commentary | No headphone monitoring on most models |
| Easy to replace if damaged | Limited dynamic range — loud exclamations may clip |

**Verdict:** If you are unsure whether you will stick with content creation, start here. The K669B is the safer choice of the two.

## 2. Mid-Range Tier ($50–150)

This is the sweet spot for serious creators. These microphones will not hold back your audio quality, and most viewers will not hear a meaningful difference between these and professional XLR setups costing three times as much.

### Blue Yeti (~$100) — Multiple Pickup Patterns, Industry Standard

- **Polar patterns:** Cardioid, bidirectional, omnidirectional, stereo — selectable via physical switch.
- **Connection:** USB (Micro-B to USB-A cable included).
- **Sample rate:** 16-bit / 48 kHz.
- **Built-in features:** Headphone jack with zero-latency monitoring, gain knob, mute button.
- **Build:** Heavy all-metal body with integrated desk stand.
- **Why it works for xiangqi:** The Yeti has been the default recommendation for podcasters and streamers for over a decade. Cardioid mode is perfect for solo commentary. If you later do a two-person analysis session, bidirectional mode picks up both speakers. The headphone jack lets you monitor yourself in real time, which helps you maintain consistent volume during long recording sessions.
- **Downsides:** Large and heavy — takes up desk real estate. The desk stand is adequate but a boom arm is strongly recommended. Picks up desk vibrations if not isolated. The Micro-B USB connector feels dated.

### HyperX QuadCast (~$130) — Built-in Pop Filter, Tap-to-Mute

- **Polar patterns:** Cardioid, bidirectional, omnidirectional, stereo.
- **Connection:** USB-C.
- **Sample rate:** 16-bit / 48 kHz.
- **Built-in features:** Internal pop filter, built-in shock mount, tap-to-mute with LED status indicator, gain dial on the bottom.
- **Why it works for xiangqi:** The tap-to-mute feature is invaluable during live streams — tap the top to instantly mute when you cough, take a drink, or need to talk to someone off-mic. The LED ring turns off when muted, giving you clear visual feedback. The internal pop filter means one fewer accessory to buy.
- **Downsides:** The red LED aesthetic is divisive. Audio quality is marginally behind the AT2020USB+ at the same price point. Software (HyperX NGENUITY) is Windows-only.

### Audio-Technica AT2020USB+ (~$150) — Studio Quality

- **Polar pattern:** Cardioid only.
- **Connection:** USB (Mini-B).
- **Sample rate:** 16-bit / 44.1 kHz.
- **Built-in features:** Headphone jack with mix control (blend mic input with computer audio), tripod desk stand included.
- **Why it works for xiangqi:** Audio-Technica is a professional audio company, and the AT2020USB+ sounds noticeably more "studio" than consumer-oriented mics. The mix control knob lets you blend your voice with game audio in your headphones without touching software — useful during live commentary.
- **Downsides:** Only cardioid — no pattern switching. The Mini-B USB connector is old. No mute button.

### Elgato Wave:3 (~$150) — Best Software Integration, Clipguard

- **Polar pattern:** Cardioid only.
- **Connection:** USB-C.
- **Sample rate:** 24-bit / 96 kHz (highest in this tier).
- **Built-in features:** Capacitive mute button, multifunction dial (gain, headphone volume, mic/PC mix), Clipguard technology.
- **Software:** Elgato Wave Link — a virtual mixer that creates separate audio channels for mic, game, music, chat, etc.
- **Clipguard:** A second, quieter audio signal that automatically replaces any clipped audio. This means if you shout "将军!" during an exciting checkmate moment, the audio stays clean instead of distorting.
- **Why it works for xiangqi:** Xiangqi commentary involves moments of calm analysis followed by bursts of excitement. Clipguard handles these dynamic shifts automatically. Wave Link software integrates beautifully with OBS, giving you granular audio control. The 24-bit recording captures more detail than 16-bit alternatives.
- **Downsides:** Requires Elgato software for full functionality. Cardioid only. The capacitive mute button can be accidentally triggered. Wave Link is macOS/Windows only.

## 5. Setup Tips for Chess Commentary

Getting the right microphone is only half the equation. Proper setup technique can make a $25 mic sound better than a $150 mic used incorrectly.

### Microphone Distance

- **Optimal range: 6–8 inches (15–20 cm) from your mouth.**
- Too close (under 4 inches): proximity effect boosts bass unnaturally, and plosives ("p" and "b" sounds, or "炮" and "兵") will pop.
- Too far (over 12 inches): the mic picks up more room reflections, and your voice sounds distant and hollow.
- Measure once, mark the position, and keep it consistent between sessions.

### Polar Pattern Selection

- **Use cardioid for solo commentary.** Cardioid picks up what is directly in front of the mic and rejects sound from the sides and rear. This means your computer fan, air conditioning, and household noise are naturally suppressed.
- Switch to bidirectional only if a second person sits across from you for a joint analysis session.
- Avoid omnidirectional unless you are recording a roundtable discussion with multiple people.

### Pop Filter

- A pop filter is a mesh screen placed between your mouth and the microphone that diffuses plosive air bursts.
- **Even a generic $5 pop filter makes a meaningful difference.** Plosive pops are one of the most common audio complaints from viewers.
- Attach it 2–3 inches in front of the mic capsule.
- Alternative: a foam windscreen that slips over the mic. Less effective than a pop filter but better than nothing.

### Boom Arm vs. Desk Stand

- **Boom arm (recommended):** Clamps to your desk edge, holds the mic in the air on an adjustable arm. Benefits:
  - Eliminates desk vibration transfer completely.
  - Positions the mic at mouth level without taking up desk space.
  - Easy to swing out of the way when not recording.
  - Generic boom arms cost $15–25 and work with all the mics listed here.
- **Desk stand:** Simpler but picks up every keyboard press and desk bump. If you use a desk stand, place a folded towel or mousepad underneath it as a basic vibration isolator.

### Testing with OBS

Before recording or streaming, verify your levels in OBS:

1. Open OBS Studio.
2. Go to **Settings → Audio**.
3. Set **Sample Rate** to 48 kHz.
4. Under **Mic/Auxiliary Audio**, select your USB microphone.
5. Close Settings. Look at the **Audio Mixer** panel at the bottom of the OBS window.
6. Speak at your normal commentary volume. The green meter should peak between **-12 dB and -6 dB**.
7. If the meter hits yellow or red (above -3 dB), reduce the mic gain — either on the mic's physical knob or in OBS (right-click the mic in the Audio Mixer → **Advanced Audio Properties** → reduce volume).
8. Apply an OBS audio filter chain for best results:
   - **Noise Suppression** (RNNoise): removes background hum and fan noise.
   - **Compressor**: evens out volume differences between quiet analysis and loud exclamations. Suggested settings: Ratio 4:1, Threshold -18 dB, Attack 6 ms, Release 60 ms.
   - **Limiter**: safety net that prevents any audio from exceeding 0 dB. Set to -1 dB.
9. Record a 60-second test clip. Play it back with headphones. Listen for clarity, background noise, and clipping.

## 6. Webcam Recommendations (Optional)

For xiangqi content, the board is the star — not your face. Most successful xiangqi channels use screen capture of a board tool with voice-over and no face-cam at all. That said, a webcam can help build personal connection with your audience.

### When a Webcam Makes Sense

- Live streams where viewer interaction matters (facial reactions to chat, reading donations).
- "Talking head" segments at the start or end of a video (introductions, conclusions).
- Tournament reaction content.

### When to Skip the Webcam

- Pre-recorded analysis videos (voice-over is sufficient and simpler to produce).
- Your first 10–20 videos (focus on content quality before adding production complexity).
- If your recording space is cluttered or poorly lit (a bad webcam image is worse than no webcam).

### Recommended Models

**Logitech C920 (~$70) — 1080p, Reliable**
- The workhorse. 1080p at 30fps, built-in stereo mics (not great, use your USB mic instead), autofocus.
- Has been the default webcam recommendation for years. Millions of reviews and troubleshooting guides exist.
- Mounts on any monitor via its built-in clip.

**Logitech C922 Pro (~$100) — Better Low-Light**
- Everything the C920 offers, plus improved low-light performance.
- Supports 720p at 60fps (smoother motion if you move around).
- Worth the premium only if your room lighting is dim.

**Elgato Facecam (~$170) — Premium**
- 1080p at 60fps, uncompressed video output, no built-in microphone (by design — forces you to use a proper mic).
- Extensive software control over exposure, white balance, field of view.
- Overkill for xiangqi commentary unless you plan to make the face-cam a major part of your brand.

**iPhone as Webcam (Free)**
- On macOS Ventura and later, Continuity Camera lets you use your iPhone as a wireless webcam. The image quality from an iPhone camera far exceeds any dedicated webcam under $200.
- Setup: iPhone and Mac on the same Apple ID → mount iPhone behind your monitor with a MagSafe mount or phone tripod → select "iPhone" as the camera source in OBS.
- On Windows: use the Camo app (free tier available) to achieve a similar result with an iPhone or Android phone.

### Webcam Placement for Xiangqi

- If you use a face-cam overlay on your board stream, place it in a corner that does not overlap with the board or engine evaluation bar. Bottom-right or top-right corners work best.
- Keep the face-cam small — 15–20% of the frame at most. The board should dominate.
- Look at the camera (or near it) when addressing the audience; look at the board when analyzing.

### When to Add a Webcam

- Wait until you have published at least 10–15 videos.
- Monitor your comments: if viewers are asking to see your face or reactions, invest in a Logitech C920 ($70) or try the iPhone-as-webcam approach first (free).
- A webcam should be a response to audience demand, not a day-one purchase.

### Upgrade Path

1. **Start:** Fifine K669B + pop filter + boom arm ($53).
2. **After 20 videos:** Upgrade to Blue Yeti or Elgato Wave:3 ($100–150). Keep the boom arm and pop filter.
3. **After 50 videos (if streaming):** Add a Logitech C920 or use your phone as a webcam.
4. **After 100 videos (if serious):** Consider an audio interface + XLR microphone (Focusrite Scarlett Solo + Audio-Technica AT2020, ~$200 total). This is the "endgame" setup — professional studio quality.

## Summary

You do not need expensive equipment to start creating xiangqi content. A $25 microphone, a $5 pop filter, proper OBS settings, and good analysis will outperform a $500 setup with poor content every time. Start with the budget kit, focus on making great commentary, and upgrade when your channel's growth justifies the investment.
