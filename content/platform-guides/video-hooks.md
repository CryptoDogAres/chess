# "First 3 Seconds" Hook Optimization Guide

A practical guide to optimizing the opening moments of every xiangqi video for maximum viewer retention across Douyin, Bilibili, and YouTube. Nail the first three seconds and the algorithm rewards you; lose them and nothing else matters.

---

## Why the First 3 Seconds Matter

### The Algorithm Reality

Every major platform measures **early retention** as the single strongest signal for whether to push your video to more viewers.

- **Douyin (抖音):** The recommendation engine weighs **completion rate** (完播率) above all other metrics. A 30-second video where 80% of viewers watch past the 3-second mark will dramatically outperform a 30-second video where only 40% do. Douyin decides within the first few hundred impressions whether your video "passes the gate." If viewers swipe away in the first 2-3 seconds, the video is dead on arrival — it will never reach the larger recommendation pool.
- **Bilibili (B站):** While B站's audience is more patient than Douyin's, the platform still tracks **average watch duration** (平均播放时长) and **retention curves** (观众留存曲线). Videos that lose more than 50% of viewers in the first 5 seconds get deprioritized in the recommendation feed. Bilibili's analytics dashboard shows you the exact second where viewers drop off — and for underperforming videos, the cliff almost always appears before the 5-second mark.
- **YouTube:** YouTube's algorithm uses **audience retention** as a core ranking factor. The internal metric YouTube cares about most is "click-through rate relative to retention." If your thumbnail earns clicks but viewers bounce in 3 seconds, YouTube interprets this as misleading content and throttles distribution. YouTube's Creator Academy explicitly states: "The first 15 seconds are critical," but internal data shows the real decision window is even shorter — around 3-5 seconds.

### The Psychological Window

Viewers make a subconscious stay-or-leave decision in roughly 2.7 seconds (based on multiple social media attention studies). This is not a rational decision. It is pattern recognition: the viewer's brain asks, "Does this look interesting enough to keep watching?" If your opening does not trigger curiosity, conflict, or recognition, they are gone.

For xiangqi content specifically, the challenge is acute. Chess positions require cognitive effort to parse. If your first frame is a confusing board with no context, the viewer's brain defaults to "too much work" and swipes away. Your hook must **lower the cognitive barrier** while simultaneously **raising curiosity**.

## 10 Hook Formulas with Xiangqi Examples

### 1. The Question Hook (问题式)

**Formula:** Ask a direct question about a board position that the viewer feels compelled to answer.

- **Chinese:** "这步棋你觉得该怎么走？"
- **English:** "What would YOU play here?"

**Why it works:** Questions activate the brain's answer-seeking instinct. The viewer cannot resist at least glancing at the board to form an opinion. Once they engage mentally, they stay to see if they were right.

**Script example:** Show a mid-game position with the camera zoomed into the critical area. Voiceover: "红方下一步怎么走？90%的人选错了。" Cut to you moving a piece after a 2-second pause.

### 2. The Bold Claim (大胆宣言式)

**Formula:** Make a superlative statement that demands proof.

- **Chinese:** "这是象棋史上最精彩的一步棋"
- **English:** "This is the most brilliant move in xiangqi history"

**Why it works:** Superlatives trigger skepticism, and skepticism drives watch time. The viewer stays to judge whether your claim holds up. Even if they disagree, they watch to the end to validate their disagreement.

**Script example:** Open on a close-up of a single piece being placed. Voiceover: "这一步棋，改变了整个象棋界。" Hold for 1.5 seconds, then zoom out to reveal the full board.

### 3. The Mistake Hook (错误揭示式)

**Formula:** Tell the viewer that most people — including them — would get this wrong.

- **Chinese:** "90%的人都会走错这步棋"
- **English:** "90% of players get this wrong"

**Why it works:** Nobody wants to be in the 90%. The viewer immediately wants to prove they are in the 10%. This creates an ego-driven retention loop: they must watch to find out the answer and validate their skill.

**Script example:** Show the board. Voiceover: "我问了50个棋友这步怎么走，只有3个人答对了。" Pause. "你觉得你能答对吗？"

### 4. The Challenge (挑战式)

**Formula:** Give the viewer a timed challenge that creates urgency.

- **Chinese:** "如果你能在10秒内找到正确答案，你就是高手"
- **English:** "If you can find the answer in 10 seconds, you're a master"

**Why it works:** Time pressure transforms passive watching into active participation. The viewer's pride is on the line. Even if they pause the video to think, that counts as engagement and extends watch time.

**Script example:** Flash the board position. Voiceover: "10秒挑战：红方怎么三步杀？计时开始。" Add a visible countdown timer overlay. After 10 seconds, reveal the solution.

### 5. The Reveal Tease (悬念预告式)

**Formula:** Promise a surprising payoff at the end without revealing what it is.

- **Chinese:** "看到最后你会明白为什么大师选择了这步"
- **English:** "By the end you'll understand why the master chose this move"

**Why it works:** This is a classic open loop. The brain hates unresolved curiosity. By promising a revelation, you create a psychological contract that the viewer feels compelled to honor by watching through.

**Script example:** Show a confusing-looking move on the board. Voiceover: "这步棋看起来完全是错的。但看完整盘棋你就会明白——这是一步天才之举。"

### 6. The Action Start (直接行动式)

**Formula:** Skip all introductions. Start with a piece moving on the board. No greeting, no context, just action.

- **Execution:** First frame is a hand (or cursor) moving a piece. Sound effect of the piece landing. Then explain.

**Why it works:** Movement captures attention reflexively. The human visual system is wired to track moving objects. By starting with action, you hijack the viewer's attention before their conscious mind decides whether to stay.

**Script example:** [No voiceover for first 2 seconds. Hand moves 炮 to a surprising position. Piece-slam sound effect.] Then: "这步炮，直接锁定胜局。"

### 7. The Emotion (情感冲击式)

**Formula:** Lead with a strong emotional reaction that makes the viewer wonder what caused it.

- **Chinese:** "我不敢相信他居然走了这步..."
- **English:** "I can't believe he actually played this..."

**Why it works:** Emotional contagion is powerful. When you express shock, disbelief, or excitement, the viewer mirrors that emotion and needs to understand its cause.

**Script example:** Open on your face (or voice) expressing genuine disbelief. "我看了三遍才确定他真的走了这步棋。" Cut to the board position. "你们自己看。"

### 8. The Comparison (对比式)

**Formula:** Present a clear contrast that highlights a skill gap or unexpected difference.

- **Chinese:** "业余 vs 专业：同样的局面，完全不同的选择"
- **English:** "Amateur vs. Pro: Same position, completely different choices"

**Why it works:** Comparisons create instant structure. The viewer knows exactly what they are about to learn and can already anticipate the "aha" moment. The gap between amateur and professional triggers both curiosity and aspiration.

**Script example:** Split screen. Left side labeled "业余选手", right side labeled "专业大师". Same board position on both. Voiceover: "你猜哪边会赢？" Then play out both lines.

### 9. The Number (数字式)

**Formula:** Lead with a specific number that implies a concrete, solvable puzzle.

- **Chinese:** "3步杀！你能看出来吗？"
- **English:** "Checkmate in 3! Can you see it?"

**Why it works:** Numbers are concrete and digestible. "3 moves" tells the viewer exactly how complex the puzzle is. It feels achievable, so they engage. Odd numbers and small numbers perform best in testing.

**Script example:** Board position fills the screen. Large text overlay: "3步杀". Voiceover: "红方先走，三步绝杀。暂停视频试试看。" Hold for 3 seconds, then begin the solution.

### 10. The Story (故事式)

**Formula:** Open with a historical or personal narrative moment that sets the stakes.

- **Chinese:** "1988年，胡荣华面对一个生死局面..."
- **English:** "In 1988, Hu Ronghua faced a life-or-death position..."

**Why it works:** Humans are story-processing machines. A narrative hook with a specific date, a real name, and high stakes triggers the "campfire response" — the instinct to keep listening to find out what happens next.

**Script example:** Black screen. Text fades in: "1988年 全国象棋个人赛 决赛". Voiceover: "胡荣华已经连续十届拿下冠军。但这一盘，他遇到了真正的危机。" Cut to the board position.

## Platform-Specific Opening Advice

### Douyin (抖音)

**NEVER start with "大家好" (Hello everyone).** This is the single most common mistake on Douyin and it is an instant swipe trigger. Douyin viewers are in rapid-scroll mode. A talking-head greeting signals "generic content" and the thumb swipes before the brain processes.

**Rules:**
- First frame must show the **chessboard**, not your face.
- Use text overlays in the first frame — large, high-contrast characters that can be read in under 1 second.
- Sound design matters: start with a piece-slamming sound effect (棋子落地声) or a dramatic music sting, never silence.
- Vertical format (9:16) means the board should fill the upper 60% of the screen with text in the lower 40%.
- Never use a fade-in from black. Douyin videos should start at full brightness, full energy, frame one.

### Bilibili (B站)

**The first frame must show the board position, not a talking head.** Bilibili's feed shows a thumbnail, but when a video autoplays in the feed, the first frame is what viewers see. A face with a microphone looks like every other vlogger. A dramatic board position signals "this is chess content" instantly and attracts the right audience.

**Rules:**
- Bilibili's audience tolerates slightly longer intros than Douyin, but "slightly" means 5 seconds, not 30.
- Use the first 3 seconds to establish the **topic and stakes**: what position, why it matters.
- Bilibili viewers appreciate context, so a 1-sentence setup is acceptable: "这是2023年全国冠军赛的一步关键棋" — then immediately show the board.
- Use 弹幕-bait questions in your opening: ask something that viewers will want to type their answer to in the bullet comments. This boosts engagement metrics.
- Include a chapter marker (进度条章节) at the 0:00 mark with a descriptive title so viewers see structure immediately.

### YouTube

**Thumbnail + first 3 seconds must match.** YouTube's algorithm punishes bait-and-switch harder than any other platform. If your thumbnail shows a dramatic board position and your video opens with 10 seconds of "Hey guys, welcome back to my channel," viewers bounce and YouTube learns that your thumbnails are misleading.

**Rules:**
- Whatever position or moment your thumbnail highlights must appear in the first 3 seconds of the video.
- Use a "cold open" structure: start with the most exciting moment, then rewind to explain context.
- English-language xiangqi content is a niche — lean into specificity. "Xiangqi puzzle" is less compelling than "The move that ended a 15-year championship streak."
- YouTube Shorts follow Douyin rules. YouTube long-form follows its own retention logic — but the first 3 seconds rule still applies to both.
- Avoid channel branding intros (animated logos, jingles). These belong at the 15-30 second mark, not at 0:00.

## 5 Bad Openings and How to Fix Them

### Bad Opening #1: The Generic Greeting

**Before:** "大家好，欢迎来到我的象棋频道，今天我们来看一个非常有意思的残局..."
(Hello everyone, welcome to my chess channel, today we're going to look at a very interesting endgame...)

**Problem:** 4 seconds of zero information. The viewer learns nothing and has no reason to stay.

**After:** "这个残局，95%的人第一步就走错。" [Show board] "红方先走，你会怎么走？"
(95% of people get the first move of this endgame wrong. Red moves first — what would you play?)

**Why it works:** Information density is immediate. A challenge is issued within 2 seconds.

### Bad Opening #2: The Slow Setup

**Before:** [10 seconds of arranging pieces on a board, camera zoomed out, no voiceover]

**Problem:** The viewer has no idea what they are about to watch or why they should care. Watching someone set up pieces is not content.

**After:** [Board already set up, camera zoomed into the critical area] "注意这个马的位置。接下来这三步，堪称教科书级别。"
(Notice where this knight is. The next three moves are textbook-level brilliant.)

**Why it works:** The board is ready, the focus is directed, and a promise is made.

### Bad Opening #3: The Throat-Clear

**Before:** "嗯...今天呢，我想给大家讲一个...就是...上次有人问我的一个残局吧。"
(Um... today, I want to talk about... well... an endgame someone asked me about last time.)

**Problem:** Hesitation and filler words signal low production value and low confidence. The viewer's patience evaporates.

**After:** "上期评论区点赞最高的问题：这个残局到底能不能赢？答案出乎你的意料。"
(The most-liked question from last episode's comments: can this endgame be won? The answer will surprise you.)

**Why it works:** References community engagement (social proof), poses a clear question, promises a surprising answer.

### Bad Opening #4: The Context Dump

**Before:** "今天我们来分析许银川和王天一在2019年全国象棋甲级联赛第七轮的一盘棋。许银川执红，王天一执黑，双方以中炮对屏风马开局..."
(Today we analyze a game between Xu Yinchuan and Wang Tianyi from round 7 of the 2019 National Xiangqi Premier League. Xu plays red, Wang plays black, they open with central cannon vs. screen horses...)

**Problem:** Too much context before any hook. The viewer needs a reason to care about these details before you deliver them.

**After:** "许银川用了一步所有人都觉得是错招的棋，结果逆转了整盘棋。" [Show the critical move] "就是这步。"
(Xu Yinchuan played a move everyone thought was a mistake — and it turned the entire game around. This move right here.)

**Why it works:** The hook comes first. The context (who, when, what tournament) can come at the 10-second mark once the viewer is committed.

### Bad Opening #5: The Apology / Disclaimer

**Before:** "这期视频画质可能不太好，因为我换了新设备还在调试，大家多包涵..."
(The video quality might not be great this time, I'm still adjusting my new equipment, please bear with me...)

**Problem:** You are literally telling the viewer to lower their expectations. They will take your advice and leave.

**After:** Simply start with good content. Fix the quality issues in editing, or acknowledge them briefly at the end if necessary. Never front-load excuses.

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before publishing any video, verify these points:

1. **Frame 1 shows the chessboard** — not your face, not a black screen, not a logo.
2. **The first sentence contains a hook** — a question, a claim, a challenge, or an emotion.
3. **No filler words in the first 5 seconds** — no "嗯", no "那个", no "好的".
4. **No greetings in the first 5 seconds** — save "大家好" for after the hook (or skip it entirely).
5. **Text overlay is present in the first frame** — reinforces the hook visually for viewers watching on mute.
6. **Sound design starts immediately** — music, sound effects, or voiceover from frame 1. No silence.
7. **The hook matches the thumbnail** — whatever promise the thumbnail makes, the first 3 seconds deliver on it.
8. **You have tested the opening** — show the first 5 seconds to someone unfamiliar with the content. If they do not want to keep watching, rewrite the hook.
